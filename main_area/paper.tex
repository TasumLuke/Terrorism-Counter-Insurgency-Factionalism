{\LARGE\bfseries Does State-Induced Insurgent Factionalism Prevent Conflict Resolution?\\[0.5em]
\large Evidence from Nagaland and Mizoram, India, 1966--2022}

\vspace{1.6cm}
{\large Rimmo Loyi Lego\\[0.2em]
\normalsize Schaefer School of Engineering, Stevens Institute of Technology,\\Hoboken, NJ, USA 07030\\[1.0em]
\large Patrick Chester\\[0.2em]
\normalsize School of Humanities, Arts \& Social Sciences, Stevens Institute of Technology,\\Hoboken, NJ, USA 07030}

\vspace{2.0cm}
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
How does rebel factionalization impact the chances for terminating conflicts, and under which circumstances can the state be seen as a cause for factionalizing rebels? This paper utilizes a natural experiment involving two insurgent movements that resemble each other structurally within the same country, namely the Mizo National Front, resolved by the 1986 Peace Accord, and the ongoing National Socialist Council of Nagaland. In this work, we develop and apply the concept of Rebel Cohesion Index, which represents a combination of measures such as faction concentration, intrarebel combat, and plurality of ceasefires. These indexes are based on annual measurements and are tested against confirmed MOHA data for 1999--2022. By using negative binomial regression, we find that the one-standard deviation decrease in rebel cohesion is linked to 33.7 percent higher chances of conflict cases per year. Our mediation analysis indicates that rebel cohesion channel accounts for 68 percent of the effect.
\end{abstract}

\vspace{0.4cm}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} insurgent factionalism, conflict termination, counterinsurgency, rebel cohesion, Nagaland, Mizoram, spoiler dynamics
\end{titlepage}

\section{Introduction}
A large amount of academic literature has shown that the internal organizational dynamics of rebel movements affect the length, strength, and eventual conclusion of civil wars \citep{cunningham2006,staniland2014,mosinger2018}. Movements that are internally cohesive will be able to commit to peace negotiations, whereas fragmented movements, divided among different factions, will produce veto players whose interests overlap and compete with each other to such an extent that it becomes impossible for a compromise to be reached \citep{cunningham2009,walter2002}. However, one important question has been overlooked until now: what occurs if, rather than allowing this organizational dynamic to occur spontaneously, the state itself seeks to create these divisions through its counter-insurgency efforts?

\section{Literature and Theory}

The hypothesis that breaking up rebel factions advances counterinsurgents' objectives has a well-developed theoretical heritage. According to \citet{galula1964}, insurgent political leaders should be separated from their military wing, and distinctions between factions should be created in order to isolate the most extreme members. Indeed, the US Army counterinsurgency doctrine includes strategies aimed at breaking up the rebel coalition \citep{fm324}. Academic literature offers empirical support for this claim. According to \citet{kalyvas2006}, selective use of force against insurgents can break up coordination and lower attack rates. \citet{lyall2009} demonstrate that indiscriminate Russian shelling in Chechnya led to a 24-percentage point reduction in the number of attacks. \citet{fjelde2012} find that state repression triggers insurgent fragmentation, while insurgency becomes militarily less effective following the occurrence of factionalization. In recent works, \citet{mosinger2018} show that the structure of civilian constituencies determines whether a rebel movement splits up or stays united, whereas \citet{fjelde2018} pinpoint conditions conducive to emergence of competing rebel factions.

However, this literature completely misses an important angle related to the temporal scope of considered outcomes. From the perspective of the peace process, the implications of fragmentation are quite different. As per \citet{cunningham2006}, each additional veto player adds to the expected length of a civil war. \citet{cunningham2009} corroborate this finding using large cross-country samples, illustrating that each additional rebel group prolongs civil war since each brings forward its own demand, which makes it unlikely that any offer will fall into the joint acceptance range of all groups. \citet{nilsson2008} argue that groups left out of incomplete peace deals become spoilers due to their material interest in derailing peace negotiations. \citet{stedman1997} gives a classic classification of spoiler behavior, noting that greedy spoilers occur much more often in fragmented civil wars. \citet{walter2002} finds that a commitment problem emerges when multiple groups hold veto powers and thus cannot bind other groups to any negotiated deal.

To fill this gap between two literatures, we separate short-term and long-term implications of state-induced factionalism in one controlled comparative analysis. In case of Nagaland, India has been able to succeed in the short term in decreasing fatalities to near zero from a peak of 213 in 2008 \footnote{See Note 11}. However, a fragmented rebel coalition precludes the possibility of a political settlement because commitment has become impossible to achieve. Meanwhile, Mizoram was able to terminate conflict after merely 20 years thanks to a lack of state-induced fragmentation of MNF.

The formal mechanism through which factionalism perpetuates conflict derives from the bargaining theory of civil war \citep{fearon1995} extended to multi-party settings by \citet{cunningham2009}. Let $F_t$ denote active insurgent factions at time $t$, and let $G_i \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k$ denote the political demand set of faction $i$. A comprehensive settlement requires an offer $s$ in the state's feasible space $S$ satisfying $s \in \bigcap_{i=1}^{F_t} G_i$. As $F_t$ increases, the probability that this intersection is non-empty declines monotonically. In Mizoram, $F_t = 1$ throughout, and the MNF's demands lay within India's offer space. In Nagaland, factional proliferation has multiplied the actors whose endorsement any compromise would require.

The second mechanism is tangible rather than symbolic. In granting a ceasefire to an armed group, the Indian government tacitly allows the group to retain its organizational structure, which consists of command structures, cadre compensation schemes, and resource extraction activities.\footnote{See Note 4.} As \citet{weinstein2007} demonstrates, material organizations, not driven by ideology but by their material gains, lack any intrinsic motivation to demobilize, since demobilization means the loss of revenue that supports the faction’s payroll. Thus, ceasefires spawn further fragmentation due to the emergence of splinter groups and further ceasefires, indefinitely postponing any prospects for peace. This is precisely the resource-conflict relationship that \citet{fearon2004} cites as a factor behind prolonged civil wars. So we hypothesize that, the State-facilitated rebel factionalism, measured through the Rebel Cohesion Index, will have a negative relationship with rebel cohesion and a positive relationship with annual incidents of conflict.

\section{Research Design}
\subsection{Case Selection}

The Naga-Mizo pair is almost an ideal Most Similar Systems design as envisioned by \citet{przeworski1970}. The two cases have similarities in terms of proximity of the two regions within a 200-kilometer radius, mostly tribal and Christian population, governance under Armed Forces Special Powers Act, backwardness as compared to India’s national average, subject to bombing by the Indian Air Force \footnote{See Note 10.}, and similar counters to insurgency. However, there is a difference in treatment, with DA referring to the case in Mizoram, where India had only one channel for negotiations and sought an accommodation, while FC refers to the case of Nagaland, where India utilized differential ceasefires and selective recognition of breakaway groups to ensure none of them became predominant.

\subsection{The Rebel Cohesion Index}

RCI or the Rebel Cohesion Index is created as a composite index measured on an annual basis for Nagaland between 1963 and 2022, and for Mizoram between 1966 and 1990, composed of three dimensions that can be independently verified. The use of the composite index for measuring conflict is consistent with the practice common to both conflict literature and literature of political economy where a multi-dimensional index is employed when the theoretical concept under study itself is multi-dimensional and cannot be measured directly by one observable variable.

First component of this index is the Organisational Fragmentation Score (OFS), which is defined to be measured as the inverse of the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) of factional cadre percentage. The HHI was developed by \citet{herfindahl1950} and \citet{hirschman1945} as a tool for measuring the degree of market concentration and has become common practice in the field of conflict studies to measure the fragmentation of rebel organizations, wherein higher concentration implies lower fragmentation:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{OFS}_t = 1 - \sum_{i=1}^{F_t} s_{it}^2, \qquad s_{it} = \frac{n_{it}}{N_t}.
\end{equation}
Values span the continuum from zero (complete unity with one faction having all the cadres) to one (the most fragmented case with numerous equal factions).\footnote{See Note 12 for sources of cadre numbers.} The OFS is assigned the greatest weight ($\alpha = 0.40$) since, in the literature on structure, the number and proportion of factions have been shown to be the key determinants of conflict length.\citet{cunningham2009} \citet{fjelde2012}.

The second component is defined as the Internecine Violence Rate (IVR), which here is taken as the share of total conflict fatalities attributable to insurgent-on-insurgent violence. This is done following the Laplace smoothing correction recommended by \citet{gelman2006} for proportions computed on small denominators:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IVR}_t = \frac{V^{\mathrm{int}}_t}{V^{\mathrm{tot}}_t + 1}.
\end{equation}
The IVR receives the second-highest weight ($\beta = 0.35$) because internecine violence is the most direct observable manifestation of the spoiler mechanism identified by \citet{stedman1997}.

The third component is taken as the Ceasefire Plurality Count (CPC), which is the number of distinct bilateral ceasefire agreements simultaneously active between India and separate factions in year $t$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CPC}_t = \sum_{i=1}^{F_t} \mathbf{1}[c_{it} = 1].
\end{equation}
The CPC directly operationalizes the veto-player structure identified by \citet{cunningham2006,cunningham2009} and herein, receives the lowest weight ($\gamma = 0.25$) because it is partly endogenous to the OFS, as new factions typically seek ceasefire recognition shortly after forming. These three components are collectively taken as parts to define how cohesive a rebel group is. All three variables together thus inturn, offer distinct elements of the concept of rebel cohesion. They include organizational coherence, the number of rebels who resort to violence against other rebels, and finally the extent to which rebel movements have more than one negotiating center. All three combined are more likely to give an accurate representation of rebel cohesion, since apart from organizational coherence, there is also the aspect of whether the groups are fighting each other as well as behaving independently in negotiations with the government.

The three components are then, min-max normalized to $[0,1]$ across the full pooled time series and combined:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RCI}_t = 1 - \bigl(\alpha\,\widehat{\mathrm{OFS}}_t + \beta\,\widehat{\mathrm{IVR}}_t + \gamma\,\widehat{\mathrm{CPC}}_t\bigr),
\qquad \alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1,
\end{equation}
where higher values indicate greater organizational cohesion.

As a validation check, the RCI also tracks the observable features of the rebel landscape with high fidelity. For instance, when the Forum for Naga Reconciliation conducted twenty-eight inter-factional mediation meetings in 2009 \citep{idsa2009}, the temporary improvement in cohesion coincided with an 85.4 percent collapse in fatalities. When the NSCN-KK formed in June 2011 and disrupted that reconciliation, the RCI declined and fatalities rebounded from 5 to 74 within two years.\footnote{See Note 9.} These within-case patterns provide face validation that the RCI, index captures the theoretical construct it is designed to measure more accurately than other individual indexes.

\subsection{Estimation Strategy}

Annual conflict incidents, which serve as the dependent variable in this study, are strongly overdispersed (with variance to mean ratio equaling 58.6 in the Nagaland series). Therefore, the Poisson distribution does not fit in this case; instead, a negative binomial estimator is used as the primary estimate \citep{cameron1998}. The negative binomial regression technique is commonly applied to modeling conflict data as it is employed by \citet{cunningham2009} when studying conflict duration counts and by \citet{lyall2009} to model insurgent attack frequencies. According to the model,
\begin{align}
Y_{ct} &\sim \mathrm{NegBin}(\mu_{ct},\,\phi), \notag\\
\ln \mu_{ct} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1\,\mathrm{RCI}_{c,t-1} + \beta_2 \ln y_{ct} + \beta_3\,\mathrm{EXT}_{ct} + \delta_c + \tau_t,
\end{align}
where $Y_{ct}$ is the annual incident count in state $c$ at year $t$, $y_{ct}$ is net state domestic product per capita,\footnote{See Note 10.} $\mathrm{EXT}_{ct}$ is a binary indicator for active cross-border insurgent support coded from annual assessments, $\delta_c$ is a state fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant state-level unobservables, and $\tau_t$ is a year fixed effect absorbing common shocks. The RCI is lagged one period to address the concern that contemporaneous incident counts might affect within-year factional composition, following the standard practice of using predetermined regressors in count-data panel models \citep{cameron1998}. Standard errors are HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust \citep{mackinnon1985}, which outperform HC0 and HC1 in the small samples characteristic of subnational conflict panels.

We supplement the primary estimator with three additional analyses. Interrupted time-series (ITS) analysis following \citet{shadish2002} tests for structural breaks at the 1986 Mizo Accord and the 1997 Nagaland ceasefire, using Newey-West HAC standard errors \citep{newey1987}. Causal mediation analysis following \citet{baronkenny1986} and \citet{imai2010} decomposes the total effect of counterinsurgency strategy on conflict intensity into the portion running through rebel organizational cohesion and the portion operating through other channels. And additionally, OLS with two-way fixed effects on $\ln(Y_{ct}+1)$ serves as a robustness check, with four alternative standard error corrections: HC3, Newey-West, state-clustered following \citet{bertrand2004}, and the wild cluster bootstrap of \citet{cameron2008}.

\section{Data and Empirical Results}
The empirical analysis draws on two primary sources: the official Ministry of Home Affairs annual insurgency series for Nagaland covering 24 years from 1999 to 2022, and a reconstructed fatality series for Mizoram's insurgency phase (1966--1990) compiled from \citet{bhaumik2009} and \citet{choudhury1999}.\footnote{See Notes 6 and 11.} Together, these yield a pooled panel of 49 state-years: 24 for Nagaland and 25 for Mizoram.
